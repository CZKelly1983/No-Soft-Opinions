The Method

Three Questions About Value

Every film is scored across seven dimensions, then run through three differently weighted frameworks — each representing a genuine philosophical position on what cinematic value means. The final score is the average of all three.

F1 — Popular Verdict
What did audiences decide?

Box office, audience devotion, and cultural footprint carry the most weight. The film the public chose, returned to, and made part of the culture.

Star Wars scores highest here. Jeanne Dielman does not.
F2 — Filmmaker's Film
What do filmmakers think?

Filmmaker influence and critical reception at release lead. The films that changed how cinema gets made — regardless of whether audiences noticed.

Jeanne Dielman scores highest here. Transformers does not.
F3 — The Long View
What does history decide?

Critical reassessment and longevity trajectory dominate. Not what the film meant in its moment, but what it means now — and whether that's rising or falling.

2001: A Space Odyssey scores highest here. Avatar does not.

For the AI-Curious

You want to see what genuine human-AI collaboration actually produces.

Not an AI answering a question. A human and an AI spending time on whether it was the right question at all. The methodology is fully visible. The limitations are named specifically — five documentaries in 1,178 films (1,104 by methodology, 74 added by hand), two Bollywood entries, the AI that has read more film criticism than any human alive and has never sat in a dark room and felt a film change something. The 74 hand-added films are the most honest thing on the site — the AI produced 1,104 and a human immediately noticed what was missing. That gap is the collaboration made visible. The gaps are findings, not failures.

Why This Exists

A Note on the Project

I built this because I was dissatisfied with what AI does when asked a simple question. Type "what's the greatest film of all time" into any AI and you get an answer — confident, pre-formed, essentially unchallengeable because there's no visible reasoning to push against.

The cinema project came from a deeper dissatisfaction than that. I didn't just want to ask the question better. I wanted to sit with the question long enough to find out whether it was the right question at all. It's the evolution of No Guilty Pleasures — British Music, which proved the methodology works. This project asks what the methodology is actually for.
